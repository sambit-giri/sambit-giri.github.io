Academic Background

My research sits at the intersection of cosmology and data science. I try to understand how profound astrophysical processes — such as the reionization of the intergalactic medium (IGM) during the first billion years, or energetic baryonic feedback during late times — affect cosmological structures and our ability to measure them.

My personal journey here has been equally dynamic. Growing up in a small Indian city, navigating the intense environment of the Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Varanasi, and eventually making the move to establish my career in Europe meant adapting to entirely new academic systems and cultures. Having lived and researched across Sweden, Switzerland, and the Netherlands, I've developed a highly adaptable, cross-cultural approach to science.

Today, I bring that global perspective to my role as an Independent Researcher at Stockholm University, leading a research program funded by the Olle Engkvists Stiftelse. My past appointments include a Nordita Fellowship and postdoctoral positions at the University of Zurich and the University of Groningen, and my work was recognized with the URSI Young Scientist Award in 2023. I am a member of the European Astronomical Society and the International Astronomical Union.

The Core of My Research

My research is driven by a single goal: extracting the maximum physical information from cosmological observations. The astrophysical processes I study — reionization of the early universe and baryonic feedback at late times — both reshape the large-scale structure of the universe, and understanding them requires progress on two fronts simultaneously. These processes demand forward models that are both physically accurate and computationally tractable; I develop machine learning emulators to accelerate complex simulations and make vast parameter space exploration feasible. At the same time, connecting these models to data demands statistical frameworks that can handle complex, high-dimensional likelihoods; I build Simulation-Based Inference (SBI) pipelines that bypass explicit likelihood computation, extracting far more information from observations than traditional two-point statistics allow.

Research Vision & Future Directions

Looking forward, the natural frontier is field-level inference — moving beyond compressed summary statistics to work directly with raw measurements of the cosmological fields. For reionization, this means synergizing 21-cm maps from the SKA, which probe the evolution of the IGM during the EoR, with high-redshift galaxy surveys from JWST, Roman, and Euclid that catalogue the sources driving reionization. For late-time structure, combining weak lensing data from Euclid with measurements of galaxy clusters will enable tighter constraints on baryonic feedback processes. Together, these multi-probe approaches will break long-standing parameter degeneracies and uncover fundamental physics across cosmic time.
